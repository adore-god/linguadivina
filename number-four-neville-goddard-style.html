A Psychological Reading of Scripture

Four: Meaning and Symbolism

The Bible is not a historical record or religious text — it is a symbolic revelation of how consciousness creates reality. Beneath the names, numbers, and stories lies a divine structure that Neville Goddard called the Law of Assumption: the principle that imagining a thing as true brings it into being. One of the clearest recurring symbols of this process is the number four.

From the four-letter name of God to the divided garments of Jesus, from the fourth sons who shape destiny to the forty-day periods of transformation, the number four appears as a sacred architecture in Scripture. This article explores how four — and its higher echo, forty — reveals the inner mechanism of assumption, manifestation, and spiritual fulfilment.

The Fourfold Structure of Consciousness

Neville Goddard described a complete framework of inner creation composed of four stages — what he called the Four Mighty Ones of man’s imagination: imagination itself, inner speech, feeling, and embodiment.

These are not external powers but functions within consciousness. They express the full journey of creation, from desire to fulfilment. Imagination initiates the act, thought supports it, feeling impresses it upon the deeper self, and finally, the world reflects it.

Neville taught that creation is finished; we do not create so much as select and assume. But for this selection to take shape, the full fourfold pattern must unfold within.

YHVH: The Four-Letter Name of Creative Law

The sacred name YHVH (יהוה) in Hebrew holds this creative pattern. It is not just a name for God but a blueprint:

  • Yod (י) – the seed, the spark of intent

  • Heh (ה) – the interior shaping through imagination

  • Vav (ו) – the emotional bridge that binds inner vision to outer world

  • Final Heh (ה) – the visible expression, the result

This sequence outlines how divine ideas become form. YHVH describes not a person, but the process by which the I AM expresses itself — in the heart of man, through the mechanism of assumption.

Daleth: The Number Four as the Door

In the Hebrew alphabet, the letter Daleth, meaning door, holds the numerical value four. It signals a threshold — a passage from inner experience into outer expression.

Daleth is the shape through which the unformed enters the formed world. Four, then, represents not simply a number but the structure that permits a desire to become flesh. It is the stabilising shape of creation, the foundation upon which manifestation rests.

The House with Many Rooms: John 14:2 and the Four Corners of Consciousness

Jesus’ words in John 14:2 — “In my Father’s house are many mansions [rooms]: if it were not so, I would have told you. I go to prepare a place for you” — provide a powerful image of the inner realm of consciousness as a structured yet expansive house.

The “Father’s house” represents the universal creative mind, the origin of all states of being. The “many rooms” or “mansions” signify the diverse states, possibilities, and manifestations within consciousness. These rooms are not chaotic but held together within a firm structure, just as a physical house is grounded by its four corners.

The four corners symbolise the sacred number four — the foundational establishment of creation. They define the boundaries and framework within which the many rooms exist. This sacred architecture reveals that while consciousness can express infinite variety (the many rooms), it is always supported and held stable by the fourfold pattern of creative law.

Thus, the number four is not a limit but a code — the divine blueprint that holds the house of consciousness intact, enabling the inner diversity Jesus describes to unfold with order and completeness.

The Role of Forty: Inner Waters of Gestation

Closely related is the number forty, represented by the Hebrew letter Mem (מ), meaning water. It denotes a period of concealment, gestation, and preparation. In Biblical narratives, forty is never random — it is always a signal that inner transformation is at work, invisible yet vital.

  • The world is cleansed and reborn after 40 days of rain.

  • Israel sheds its identity during 40 years in the wilderness.

  • Moses receives divine law after 40 days on the mountain.

  • Jesus affirms his inner authority after 40 days of fasting.

This period of forty is the hidden interval — not delay, but development. When the door (Daleth) of assumption is stepped through, what follows is not immediate revelation, but immersion in Mem — the inner waters of becoming. This is the sacred space where the seed, having been planted in imagination, is nourished in stillness before emerging into visible reality.

The Fourth Son: Praise, Fulfilment, and Breakthrough

Biblical birth orders carry spiritual meaning. The fourth son often marks the pivot from longing to realisation.

Leah, who bore sons out of sorrow and desire, gives birth to her fourth and says, “Now I will praise the Lord.” She names him Judah, meaning praise. With Judah, she ceases striving and moves into a state of thanksgiving — a posture that mirrors Neville’s teaching: live as though the assumption is already fulfilled.

Judah becomes the line through which kings are born — David, Solomon, and ultimately Jesus. Though not first in birth, he is first in spiritual priority. The fourth is the moment when inner knowing becomes settled.

Later in this line, Perez is born — a child whose name means breakthrough. He unexpectedly emerges before his brother, reversing the natural order. This shows how manifestation often occurs: not according to logic, but by divine disruption. What seemed slow or secondary suddenly emerges as first.

Jesus’ Seamless Garment and the Fourfold World

At the crucifixion, Jesus’ garments - a symbol of the mind dressed in its new ideal state - are divided into four parts, one for each soldier. Yet his inner tunic, woven in one piece, is not divided.

This imagery speaks volumes. The outer self, the expressions of daily life,.is seen as split into four, reflecting the fragmented nature of experience. But the inner identity, the true I AM, remains whole and undivided.

Jesus’ seamless garment is not only historical, but symbolic of the unbroken consciousness beneath all form. The fourfold world may appear divided, but the self within — the imaginative awareness — is singular, indivisible, and complete

Four Across the Scriptures: A Pattern of Completion

The pattern of four is repeated throughout Scripture as a sign of divine wholeness:

  • The four rivers of Eden, flowing from one source

  • The four living creatures around the throne

  • The four horns of the altar, representing universal reach

  • The four corners of the earth, symbolising total influence

  • The four gospels, each presenting one face of the same revelation

  • The four soils in the parable, reflecting conditions of the human heart

Each set of four points to the same idea: that divine intention finds complete expression in four. It represents the framework through which God — in the imagination of man — moves from invisibility to incarnation.

Conclusion: Four and Forty as the Rhythm of Creation

The number four reveals the design, the doorway through which spirit becomes flesh. The number forty reveals the mystery - the waters of quiet transformation that follow once the door is entered.

Together, they form the full arc of manifestation:

  • The structure of imagination (four)

  • The immersion in unseen process (forty)

  • The sudden breakthrough into form (Perez)

  • The wholeness beneath division (Jesus’ garment)

  • The final embodiment of I AM in visible world

This is not mysticism for mysticism’s sake. It is the law of assumption written in number, form, and story. The Bible encodes it again and again: enter the inner door, walk with unwavering awareness, and trust the invisible waters to deliver what has already been conceived within.

The sacred four is not a symbol of limitation — but of completion. And from within it, the fullness of life takes shape.
